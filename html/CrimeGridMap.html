# Binning Crime into Hex Bins In the previous plot we examined a dot map showing a subset of victim-based crimes (common assault, aggravated assault) committed in Baltimore during 2014. While that example was a good initial visualization, it was less than ideal. Points are densely backed and it it difficult to see trends at a glance. To take a step toward better visualizing this data into a more useful format we will bin the data into hexagonal bins and visualize those bins. I've divided the original data into groups: violent and non-violent. The violent group contains instances of aggravated assault, common assault, and shootings. The non-violent group contains burglary, larceny and larceny from auto. Other groups present in the original data were excluded. In this case I will use all of the data included in the file, consisting of crimes committed between Janurary 1, 2010 and February 21, 2015. The violent group included 70,347 records while the non-violent group included 128,112 records. **Generation of this plot required the use of Python, Pandas, QGIS, TileMill, Mapbox, Javascript, D3 and Leaflet.** ## Demo In this map, two grids are present, one for non-violent crimes and one for violent crimes. The map has been set up so that each layer of grid cells may be toggled. #### Non-violent crimes Counts of non-violent crimes are indicated by cell colors varying linearly from **pale blue (15 to 34 incidents)** to **dark blue (105 or more incidents)**. Regions of high non-violent crime appear in a Northern-Southern strip in central Baltimore. Thie strip corresponds to areas with a high density of shops, real estate with above-average property values and several main traffic routes for the city. An additional cluster is present in Eastern Baltimore in a poorer region of the city. This particular cluster seems to coincide with a cluster of violent crime. #### Violent crimes Counts of violent crimes are indicated by cell colors varying linearly from pale orange **(5 to 14 incidents)** to **dark orange (55 or more incidents)**. From this layer we can see that regions with high incidence rates of violent crime are located in 3 primary areas: a small patch in Eastern Baltimore, a more dispersed patch in Western Baltimore and a central patch near the harbor. The Eastern and Western clusters correspond to areas with comparatively less economic development and typically lower household incomes. As an example the "coordinates" column that was in the original data contained latitude and longitude in the form of "(lat, lon)" stored as a string. This column was stripped of unnecessary characters, split into two separate columns for latitude and longitude, then converted from strings to floats. Additionally, entries containing latitude and longitude values out of range or with values of NaN were removed. Finally, a categorical value column was created to hold integers corresponding to each of the crime types in the file. This would enable easier selection of the appropriate records outside of Pandas. A hexagonal grid of polygons with center to center horizontal separation of 0.0015 decimal degrees and vertical separation of ~0.0018 decimal degrees was generated using the MMQGIS plugin. Queries were written to divide crime data was divided into separate layers corresponding to the violent and non-violent categories previously specified. Hexagonal grids were created for each layer and a QGIS built-in point-in-polygon procedure was used to identify the number of counts in each hexagonal bin. These counts were appended as an attribute table associated with each layer. The binned layers and tables were exported as ESRI shapefiles to prepare for visual styling. #### Visual Styling of Data The shapefiles containing the gridded crime counts were imported into TileMill. TileMill is software for styling geometric map data and generating raster tiles for use on map servers. Separate blue and orange color gradients from [ColorBrewer](http://colorbrewer2.org/) were generated for coloring the non-violent and violent crime data, respectively. Basic styling code was written in CartoCSS, a styling language for maps.
